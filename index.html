What is an IDMT relay?

An IDMT (Inverse Definite Minimum Time) relay is a protection relay where the trip time is inversely proportional to the fault current — the higher the fault current, the faster it trips. They are widely used in low and medium voltage power systems to provide overcurrent protection and coordinate with other protective devices.

What is TMS in a protection relay?

TMS stands for Time Multiplier Setting (IEC) and TD stands for Time Dial (IEEE). Both are scaling factors applied to the relay's trip time curve, allowing engineers to coordinate multiple relays in a protection scheme. A higher TMS/TD results in a longer trip time for a given fault current, creating discrimination between upstream and downstream relays.

What is the difference between IEC 60255 and IEEE C37.112 curves?

IEC 60255 and IEEE C37.112 are different international standards defining inverse time overcurrent characteristics. IEC curves are used in Australia, the UK and Europe. IEEE curves are used primarily in North America. Both use different mathematical constants (k/p for IEC, A/B/p for IEEE) but achieve the same goal of time-current discrimination.

What is relay pickup current?

The relay pickup current (Is) is the minimum fault current at which the relay will operate. The fault current must exceed the pickup current for the relay to trip. The ratio of fault current to pickup current (I/Is) is called the current multiple (M) and must be greater than 1 for the relay to operate.

Which IEC relay curve should I use?

Standard Inverse is the most common general-purpose curve. Very Inverse suits overhead lines where fault current varies with distance. Extremely Inverse is ideal where the source impedance is high. Long Time Inverse suits motors and transformers with high inrush. Ultra Inverse provides the sharpest discrimination at high fault currents.
